Guidance

What happens on a [scheme name] visit

Guidance on what happens when a vet visits your farm to carry out a livestock health and welfare review and endemic disease follow-up.

From: Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs and Rural Payments Agency

Applies to England

Contents

  1. Livestock health and welfare review
  2. Endemic disease follow-ups
  3. Get help with your visit

Livestock health and welfare review

This is the first part of the [scheme name] which will help asess the health and condition of your livestock. You must have an agreement in place before the vet visits your farm to carry out a livestock health and welfare review or endemic disease follow-up.

What you must ask the vet to do

  1. Visit your farm and take samples for the recommended testing of the one livestock type that you’re having reviewed:

    • bovine viral diarrhoea (BVD) in dairy and beef cattle
    • effectiveness of worming treatments in sheep
    • porcine reproductive and respiratory syndrome (PRRS) in pigs

  2. Advise on the health and welfare of these livestock - examples could include lameness or body condition.

  3. Give you a written report after the review, including recommended follow-up actions or other observed symptoms or condtions.

  4. Give you a summary of the review that you can use as evidence if the Rural Payments Agency (RPA) requests it when you claim.

How long the review takes

There’s no set amount of time for a review, but it’s likely to take between 2 and 3 hours of your time and the vet’s time. You may ask the vet to spread it over several regular visits to the farm or do it in a single visit.

Discuss the health and welfare of your livestock

You should discuss any issues on your farm and the vet can make recommendations on how to help and what to do next. Examples may include reviewing biosecurity and medicine usage, and discussing any disease concerns you have. Your vet can advise you on what to focus on.

During the review farm visit

During the visit, you must ask the vet to carry out sampling for the required testing, and to give you health and welfare advice about your livestock. You may also discuss topics such as biosecurity or medicine usage.

You can:

  • use these review test results for other accreditation schemes you’re a member of - you’ll need to check with the accreditation scheme that they will accept testing from the review
  • schedule your review to fit in with your existing testing schedule if you’re already testing for these diseases or conditions to take advantage of the funding

You cannot:

  • use other accreditation scheme test results for this review funding
  • ask the vet to test for other medical conditions or diseases as part of the review
  • use testing from on or after your agreement start date. This must be during or after the first time the vet visits your farm, and before you claim

You’ll only be eligible for funding if the vet:

  • samples the required number of animals for the type of review they’re doing
  • gets samples tested at a laboratory that meets the standard for review testing

To find out more about testing including the minimum required numbers and specifications a laboratory needs to meet, read the guidance:

  • cattle: testing required
  • sheep: testing required
  • pigs: testing required

After the review

Before you can claim your funding, you must ask the vet to give you a written report and a summary document which you can use as evidence that the review has taken place.

Get a written report

The report should be a specific report for the review.

Your report from the vet should include:

  • results from the laboratory of any tests carried out as part of this reveiw
  • advice or suggested species-specific health and welfare actions
  • other review findings - for example, biosecurity recommendations and medicine usage
  • agreed achievable actions to address issues raised by the review or testing - likely to be 2 to 3 but can be more or less based on individual needs

The Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) will not ask to see the report.

Get a vet summary

The RPA may ask you to supply evidence that the review took place when you claim.  

Ask the vet for a summary of the review that you can use as evidence. The summary must be a separate document. It can be a digital or paper version.

The vet’s summary must include:

  • the agreement number
  • the business name and SBI number where the review took place
  • the date of the vet’s last visit to the farm for the review
  • the type of livestock reviewed
  • confirmation the minimum number of animals required for the scheme were on the farm when the vet did the review
  • confirmation the vet carried out testing in accordance with the review’s endemic disease or condition testing guidance
  • the date the vet completed endemic disease or condition testing
  • the number of beef cattle, sheep and pigs the vet tested - you do not need to provide the number of dairy cattle tested
  • the date the vet completed endemic disease sampling or wormer treatment check
  • the unique reference number for laboratory test results
  • the vet’s name and Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons (RCVS) number
  • confirmation the vet has given you a written report
  • vet signature and date document signed

You can also ask the vet to include a vet attestation number (VAN) on the summary.

You’ll need this number if one or both of the following enter the food chain and may be exported to the EU:

  • livestock you produce
  • animal products derived from your livestock

You must ask the vet to sign and date the summary. The signature can be digital or on paper.

Endemic disease follow-ups

This is the second part of the [scheme name] which will build on the review and identify which animals have diseases and how to eradicate them.

Timing of vet visits

If you have already had a livestock health and welfare review, all your reviews and follow-ups must be done on the same species tested during your first review.

If you are new to the scheme, any endemic disease follow-up must be done on the same species as your review.

There must be:

  • at least 10 months between the date of your vet visits for each livestock health and welfare review
  • at least 10 months between your endemic disease follow-ups
  • no more than 10 months between your health and welfare reviews and subsequent endemic disease follow-ups

How long endemic disease follow-ups take

There is no set time for an follow-up, though it’s likely to take between 2 and 3 hours for the biosecurity assessment and testing to be completed. This could all happen in the same visit or could be spread across several visits.

What you must ask the vet to do

  1. Visit your farm for a follow-up and take samples from your livestock. The samples will differ depending on which livestock species the endemics disease follow-up is for. Samples will not always be required for sheep, [read the vet guidance] for more information on testing and sampling.

  2. Complete [biosecurity assessment]

  3. Send test samples to a laboratory that meets the follow-up standard for testing.

  4. Give you a written report with the endemic disease or condition test results and discuss the follow-up actions to improve the biosecurity score or assessment.

  5. Give you a summary of the endemics disease follow-up that you can use as evidence if the Rural Payments Agency (RPA) requests it when you claim.

Required testing

The testing that happens in the follow-up will differ depending on the species you have chosen and the result of your livestock health and welfare review.

You must make sure the vet completes the required testing or sampling. You can [read the vet guidance] to learn more about what the vet needs to do.

After the endemic disease follow-ups

Before you can claim your funding, you must ask the vet to give you a written report and a summary document you can use as evidence that the review has taken place.

Get a written report

For dairy cattle, beef cattle and pigs, the report must include the:

  • vet’s biosecurity score or assessment
  • recommended actions to improve the biosecurity score or assessment
  • relevant endemic disease and/or condition test results
  • recommended actions to take to improve the disease or condition situation on farm any other agreed, achievable actions that should be taken by the farmer.
  • This is likely to be 2-3 actions but could be more or less depending on the individual farm’s needs

Get a vet summary

Your vet must give you a summary of the follow-up visit. The summary must include all the details that you will need to make a claim for funding including:

  • your agreement number
  • the business name and SBI number where the follow-up took place
  • the date of the vet’s last visit to the farm for the follow-up
  • the type of livestock reviewed
  • confirmation that the minimum number of animals required for the scheme were on the farm at the time the vet did the follow-up
  • confirmation the vet carried out sampling in accordance with the endemic disease or condition follow-up guidance
  • the date the vet completed follow-up sampling
  • the unique reference number for laboratory test results
  • test results from any follow-up testing
  • confirmation that the vet has carried out a biosecurity assessment and for pigs only, an assessment score
  • vaccination status and herd disease status number (for pigs only)
  • the vet’s name and Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons (RCVS) number
  • confirmation that the vet has given you a written report
  • vet signature and date document signed

The RPA may ask to see the vet summary as evidence that the follow-up took place.

Have your agreement number with you when you call or include it in your email.
