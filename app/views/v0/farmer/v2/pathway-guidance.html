Get funding for a vet visit

The Annual Health and Welfare Review (AHWR) will pay you to cover the costs of a vet visit on your farm.

Overview

The funding will cover:

  • a yearly visit from a vet or vet-led team
  • diagnostic testing for risks like Bovine Viral Diarrhoea
  • advice on how to improve livestock health and welfare

Who is eligible

You’re eligible for funding if you’re a commercial cattle, pig and sheep keeper who has:

  • received BPS (direct payments) in the last 12 months
  • at least one of the following:
    • 10 cattle
    • 20 sheep
    • 50 pigs

Payments

Payments will range from £269 to £681, depending on the type of livestock you have, and if you’re part of a farm assurance scheme or not.

Dairy cattle on the Red Tractor scheme – £269

All other cattle – £483

Sheep – £488

Pigs – £681

What you need to do

Once you apply for funding, you’ll:

  • need to contact your vet to arrange a visit
  • pay the vet

Following the vet visit, it’ll usually take Defra about 28 days to send the funding. You may need to pay the vet before you get your funding.

What the vet will do

A vet’s actions and recommendations will depend on the type of livestock you have and the circumstances of your farm.

These activities might be completed in a single visit, but this will depend on your farm’s circumstances.

The vet will work with you to agree on the best actions for your farm. This means they will carry out some or all the actions listed, depending on the needs of the animals and the farm.

Cattle – vet actions

This applies to farm-assured and non-farm assured cattle.

1. Biosecurity review

The vet will check on your current biosecurity measures. This means measures you’re taking to stop disease entering or leaving the farm.

They’ll then give advice on how to improve biosecurity and will set out recommendations for any changes you need to make.

They’ll also give you advice on grants you could apply for to cover biosecurity costs.

2. Farm medicine use review

The vet will record all the medicines used on farm, including all anti-microbial usage. They will make recommendations on any changes needed.

3. Whole herd lameness assessment

The vet will check lameness in the herd and make recommendations in a herd health and welfare plan (HHWP). They will also check progress on the farm against lameness reduction targets.

4. Herd health and welfare review

The vet will work with you to review the health and welfare of the herd and make recommendations.

The vet’s review will set out:

  • health and welfare issues identified as part of the visit
  • recommended investigations to be carried out to make AHWP progress
  • what support (e.g. government grants, industry-led schemes etc) are available
  • ways to solve health and welfare issues identified in the review

5. Bovine Viral Diarrhoea (BVD) control

The vet will collect samples to check if your farm has evidence of an active BVD virus, and will act in line with existing BVD control and eradication schemes (opens in new tab).

They will also give recommendations on how to manage risk of BVD. These recommendations will vary depending on the type of cattle you have.

6. Advice on access to grants and payments

This will allow the vet to support your decisions on what grants and other funding you can apply for to benefit your herd’s health and welfare.

Sheep – vet actions

This applies whether you’re in a farm assurance scheme or not.

1. Review on farm medicine use

The vet will carry out the review to understand the medicines used on farm, including all antimicrobial usage. They’ll then make the appropriate recommendations to you.

The vet will also discuss how to reduce antimicrobial use through alternative methods to control and treat disease. They’ll also tell you how to submit antimicrobial use data to the using the Agriculture and Horticulture Development Board (AHDB) Hub (opens in new tab).

If you’re not yet using the AHDB Medicine Hub, you must:

  • sign up the Hub
  • use it throughout the next 12 months
  • carry out a review of antibiotic use, using existing farm and vet records

2. Testing for resistance to parasite treatments (anthelmintics)

The vet will carry out a Faecal egg count reduction test (FECRT) on between 15 and 20 sheep to check for resistance to parasite treatment in the farm. This forms part of Defra’s Sustainable Control of Parasite (SCOPS).

FECs are then repeated after 7 or 14 days (this will depend on which treatment is being used in the flock.

The vet will discuss the results and treatment options with you as part of their visit.

3. Biosecurity review

The vet will check on your current biosecurity measures. This means measures you’re taking to stop disease entering or leaving the farm.

They’ll then give advice on how to improve biosecurity and will set out recommendations for any changes you need to make.

They’ll also give you advice on grants you could apply for to cover biosecurity costs.

4. Health and welfare data collection

The vet will collect this data as part of their goals to improve the understanding of the health and welfare of the national flock.

The data relates to:

  • flock mortality
  • antimicrobial usage
  • anthelmintic resistance
  • biosecurity score

5. Advice on access to grants and payments

This will allow the vet to support your decisions on what grants and other funding you can apply for to benefit your herd’s health and welfare.

Pigs – vet actions

This applies whether you’re in a farm assurance scheme or not.

1. Biosecurity review

The vet will check on your current biosecurity measures. This means measures you’re taking to stop disease entering or leaving the farm.

They’ll then give advice on how to improve biosecurity and will set out recommendations for any changes you need to make.

2. Testing for presence of diseases of economic importance

This will initially cover the Porcine reproductive and respiratory syndrome virus (PRRSv).

The vet will use the data to give recommendations on how to carry out disease control.

3. Herd health and welfare review

The vet will work with you to review the health and welfare of the herd and make recommendations.

The vet’s review will set out:

  • health and welfare issues identified as part of the visit
  • recommended investigations to be carried out to make AHWP progress
  • available support on ways to solve health and welfare issues

4. Advice on access to grants and payments

This will allow the vet to support your decisions on what grants and other funding you can apply for to benefit your herd’s health and welfare.

How to apply

Apply for funding, it takes around 5 minutes.

Contact

If you cannot apply online or have a question about your application, contact:

Rural Payments Agency
ruralpayments@defra.gov.uk
Telephone: 01900 508 622
Monday to Friday, 8:30am to 5pm
